Learning Objective 17‑3

Financial Reporting

Understand the two required layers of governmental financial reporting and how fund-based statements differ from government-wide statements.

Two required layers of reporting

Under GASB 34, every state and local government must produce two distinct layers of financial statements. They are not alternatives — both are required, and together they give a complete picture of a government's finances that neither could provide alone.

Fund-Based Statements

Answer: "Did each fund operate within its legal and budgetary constraints?"

  • Report each individual fund separately
  • Primary objective: demonstrate fiscal accountability
  • Governmental funds use modified accrual accounting
  • Focus on current financial resources only
  • Do not include capital assets or long-term debt
  • Residual called fund balance
  • Operating statement reports expenditures
Government-Wide Statements

Answer: "What is the overall financial health of the government?"

  • Report the government as a single economic unit
  • Primary objective: demonstrate operational accountability
  • Always use full accrual accounting
  • Focus on all economic resources
  • Include capital assets (with depreciation) and long-term debt
  • Residual called net position
  • Operating statement reports expenses

Which fund types use which statements?

Not all funds are reported the same way. The key distinction is between governmental funds on one side, and proprietary and fiduciary funds on the other.

Governmental Funds General, Special Revenue, Capital Projects, Debt Service, Permanent

Governmental funds use fund-based statements only. They present a balance sheet and a statement of revenues, expenditures, and changes in fund balance under modified accrual. Capital assets and long-term debt do not appear here — they are picked up and converted to full accrual in the government-wide statements.

Proprietary Funds Enterprise, Internal Service

Proprietary funds use the same basis as the government-wide statements — full accrual, all economic resources, net position. Their fund-level statements (statement of net position, statement of revenues/expenses, and statement of cash flows) are essentially already in government-wide format. No conversion is needed.

Fiduciary Funds Trust Funds, Agency Funds

Fiduciary funds also use full accrual, consistent with the government-wide approach. However, because fiduciary resources belong to others and cannot be used for the government's own operations, fiduciary funds are excluded from the government-wide statements entirely. They are reported only in their own fund-level statements.

Why two layers?

Each layer serves a different audience asking a different question.

Fund-based statements serve citizens, legislators, and oversight bodies who need to know whether public money was collected and spent in accordance with the law. Did the general fund stay within its budget? Were restricted grant dollars spent only on authorized purposes? These questions are answered at the fund level.

Government-wide statements serve analysts, bondholders, and anyone evaluating the government's long-run financial sustainability. What does the city truly owe, including pension obligations and infrastructure debt? Are its roads and bridges depreciating faster than they're being replaced? These questions require a full economic picture — one that fund statements, by design, don't provide.

The two layers complement rather than duplicate each other. A government could look healthy in its fund statements (balanced budget, positive fund balance) while simultaneously deteriorating in its government-wide position (aging infrastructure, growing pension liabilities).

What each layer includes

Fund-Based Balance Sheet
  • Cash and short-term receivables
  • Prepaid expenses and inventories
  • Short-term payables
  • Fund balance (nonspendable, restricted, assigned, unassigned)
  • Not included: capital assets, long-term debt
Government-Wide Statement of Net Position
  • All assets above, plus capital assets net of depreciation
  • All liabilities above, plus long-term debt and pension obligations
  • Net position (net investment in capital assets; restricted; unrestricted)
  • Includes: everything on the fund balance sheet, plus long-term items
Fund-Based Operating Statement
  • Revenues (when measurable and available)
  • Expenditures (full cost when goods/services received)
  • Other financing sources and uses
  • Change in fund balance
Government-Wide Statement of Activities
  • Program revenues and expenses by function
  • Depreciation expense on capital assets
  • General revenues (taxes, grants not restricted to programs)
  • Change in net position

A quick example: the same road, two treatments

Barb City spends $2,000,000 to repave a major road. Here is how each layer handles it:

Fund-Based (Capital Projects Fund)

The $2,000,000 is recorded as an expenditure in the year the road is paved. The road itself does not appear as an asset on the fund balance sheet. The fund's balance drops by $2,000,000.

Government-Wide

The $2,000,000 is capitalized as an infrastructure asset on the statement of net position. Each year, a portion is recorded as depreciation expense on the statement of activities, spreading the cost over the road's useful life.

Same transaction, two very different pictures. The fund statement shows a big outflow this year; the government-wide statement shows a long-lived asset gradually consumed over time. Neither view is "wrong" — they serve different purposes.

Which of the following items would appear on a government‑wide statement of net position but NOT on a governmental fund balance sheet?

Correct answer: d — Government‑wide statements include capital assets like infrastructure. Governmental fund balance sheets focus only on current financial resources and exclude long‑term capital items.

The primary purpose of government‑wide financial statements is to:

Correct answer: a — Government‑wide statements demonstrate operational accountability — the big‑picture economic health of the government as a whole.

Barb City spends $3,000,000 repaving roads. How does the governmental fund treat this transaction?

Correct answer: b — Governmental funds record the full cost of capital outlays as an expenditure in the year incurred. The road does not appear as an asset on the fund balance sheet.
