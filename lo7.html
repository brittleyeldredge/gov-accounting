Learning Objective 17‑7

Journal Entries for the General Fund

Make calculations and record journal entries for the general fund.

Beyond the basics — specific situations in the general fund

In LO 6 we walked through the core budget and encumbrance cycle that drives most of a government's day-to-day accounting. But the fiscal year is twelve months long, and real governments encounter situations that don't fit neatly into the basic pattern. What happens when an order hasn't arrived by June 30 and the books need to close? How do you account for a stockroom full of supplies? What about a new fire truck, a bond issuance, or a portfolio of investments?

This section works through those specific situations — all still within the general fund — and shows exactly how the journal entries handle each one. As you work through these, keep the modified accrual framework in mind: we are always asking whether a resource is current, measurable, and available, and whether a liability is payable from current financial resources.

Outstanding encumbrances at year‑end

Governments aren't required to honor open encumbrances from the prior year — but in practice they almost always rebudget and honor them. Two accounting methods exist:

Option 1 — Encumbrances Lapse
  • Closed at year‑end; amount set aside in assigned fund balance.
  • Encumbrance is reestablished at the start of the new period to await delivery.
Option 2 — Encumbrances Don't Lapse
  • Closed at year‑end; amount set aside in assigned fund balance.
  • Encumbrance is not reestablished — a portion of fund balance stays assigned for prior‑year encumbrances until delivery.

Worked example: both options side by side

On February 1, 20X2, Barb City orders $15,000 of supplies and records an encumbrance. The supplies have not arrived by June 30, 20X2 (fiscal year‑end). They are delivered on August 1, 20X2 at an actual cost of $14,000.

February 1, 20X2 — Order placed (same for both options)

February 1, 20X2 — Record encumbrance for supplies ordered
AccountDebitCredit
ENCUMBRANCES15,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000

June 30, 20X2 — Year‑end close (same for both options)

Both options close the budgetary accounts and reclassify fund balance identically at year-end. The paths diverge on July 1.

June 30, 20X2 — Close the open encumbrance at year‑end
AccountDebitCredit
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000
ENCUMBRANCES15,000
June 30, 20X2 — Reserve fund balance for the outstanding commitment
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Unassigned15,000
Fund Balance—Assigned for Encumbrances15,000

Option 1 — Encumbrance Lapses

On July 1, the encumbrance is reestablished in the new year's budgetary accounts, and the fund balance reclassification from June 30 is reversed — the budgetary accounts now signal the commitment, so the assigned fund balance is no longer needed.

July 1, 20X2 — Reestablish encumbrance in new period
AccountDebitCredit
ENCUMBRANCES15,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000
July 1, 20X2 — Release the assigned fund balance back to unassigned
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Assigned for Encumbrances15,000
Fund Balance—Unassigned15,000

When goods arrive on August 1, reverse the encumbrance and record the expenditure:

August 1, 20X2 — Reverse encumbrance and record expenditure
AccountDebitCredit
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000
ENCUMBRANCES15,000
Expenditures14,000
Vouchers Payable14,000

At June 30, 20X3 (year-end), close the budgetary accounts and close expenditures to fund balance:

June 30, 20X3 — Close budgetary accounts
AccountDebitCredit
APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL850,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—UNASSIGNED50,000
ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL900,000
June 30, 20X3 — Close revenues and expenditures to fund balance
AccountDebitCredit
Revenues880,000
Expenditures820,000
Fund Balance—Unassigned60,000

Option 2 — Encumbrance Does Not Lapse

On July 1, the fund balance is reclassified to a prior year account. No encumbrance is reestablished.

July 1, 20X2 — Reclass fund balance to prior year designation
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Assigned for Encumbrances15,000
Fund Balance—Assigned for Encumbrances—PY15,000

When goods arrive on August 1, record the expenditure as a prior year item:

August 1, 20X2 — Record prior year expenditure on delivery
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures—PY14,000
Vouchers Payable14,000

At June 30, 20X3 (year-end), release the PY fund balance, close budgetary accounts, and close expenditures to fund balance:

June 30, 20X3 — Release the prior year assigned fund balance
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Assigned for Encumbrances—PY15,000
Fund Balance—Unassigned15,000
June 30, 20X3 — Close budgetary accounts
AccountDebitCredit
APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL850,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—UNASSIGNED50,000
ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL900,000
June 30, 20X3 — Close revenues and expenditures to fund balance
AccountDebitCredit
Revenues880,000
Expenditures—PY14,000
Expenditures806,000
Fund Balance—Unassigned60,000

Both options end with the same $14,000 expenditure in 20X2‑X3. The key distinction is what happens on July 1 — Option 1 reestablishes the encumbrance in the new year's budgetary accounts; Option 2 reclassifies the fund balance to a prior year designation and waits for delivery without any budgetary entry. The $1,000 difference between the original encumbrance ($15,000) and the actual cost ($14,000) returns to unassigned fund balance either way.

Expenditures for inventory: two methods

Purchase Method

Records the entire expenditure when inventory is acquired. Treats acquisition as the outflow of resources.

Consumption Method

Records an expenditure only for inventory actually used. Treats usage as the outflow of resources.

Both are accepted in practice. The choice depends on the unit's budgeting policy. Either way, balance sheet amounts are unaffected — only the period of the expenditure differs.

Worked example: purchase vs. consumption

Barb City acquires $2,000 of inventory on May 1, 20X2 (no prior inventory). At June 30, 20X2 (year‑end), $1,400 remains on hand. During 20X3, $900 is used, leaving $500 on June 30, 20X3.

May 1, 20X2 — Acquire inventory (same entry for both methods)
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures2,000
Cash / Vouchers Payable2,000

Purchase Method

The full $2,000 expenditure stands. At year‑end, the $1,400 still on hand is recognized as an asset and fund balance is reclassified as nonspendable.

June 30, 20X2 — Record inventory on hand as an asset
AccountDebitCredit
Inventory of Supplies1,400
Fund Balance—Nonspendable1,400

20X3: $900 of inventory is used. No expenditure entry — it was already expensed at purchase. At year‑end, reduce inventory from $1,400 to $500 and release nonspendable fund balance.

June 30, 20X3 — Reduce inventory to reflect usage ($900 consumed)
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Nonspendable900
Inventory of Supplies900

Inventory balance: $1,400 − $900 = $500. Fund Balance—Nonspendable reduced to match.

Consumption Method

Only $600 was consumed ($2,000 − $1,400 on hand). Expenditures are reduced and the unused inventory is reclassified. Fund balance is also reclassified since inventory is nonspendable.

June 30, 20X2 — Reduce expenditures to amount consumed, record inventory
AccountDebitCredit
Inventory of Supplies1,400
Expenditures1,400
June 30, 20X2 — Reclassify fund balance for nonspendable inventory
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Unassigned1,400
Fund Balance—Nonspendable1,400

20X3: $900 of inventory is used. Record the expenditure as it is consumed, then adjust fund balance at year‑end.

During 20X3 — Record expenditure as inventory is consumed
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures900
Inventory of Supplies900
June 30, 20X3 — Release nonspendable fund balance for inventory consumed
AccountDebitCredit
Fund Balance—Nonspendable900
Fund Balance—Unassigned900

Inventory balance: $1,400 − $900 = $500. Fund Balance—Nonspendable reduced to match.

Both methods end with the same $500 inventory balance at June 30, 20X3. The difference is timing: the purchase method expenses the full cost upfront and adjusts the balance sheet at year‑end; the consumption method keeps expenditures aligned with actual usage each period.

Accounting for fixed assets

  • Governmental funds — recognize the cost of acquiring capital assets as an expenditure in the year acquired. No asset is recorded on the fund‑based balance sheet.
  • Proprietary funds — capitalize and depreciate like a commercial entity.
  • Works of art & historical treasures — capitalized at historical cost (or fair value at date of donation) in the government‑wide statements. Donated assets generate contribution revenue there.

Long‑term debt

Bond issuance in a governmental fund
AccountDebitCredit
Cashxxx
Bond Issue Proceeds (other financing source)xxx
  • Proceeds are not revenue — the bonds must be repaid.
  • Bonds do not appear on the governmental fund's balance sheet — only in the government‑wide statements.

Finance leases

Proprietary Fund

Record a right‑of‑use asset and lease liability, like a commercial entity.

Governmental Fund

Accounted for like long‑term debt — proceeds treated as an other financing source; no long‑term liability on the fund balance sheet.

Investments

  • GASB 31 — general rule of fair market valuation for government investments.
  • Debt securities, equity securities, open‑end mutual funds, investment pools, and interest‑earning investment contracts are valued at fair value (if determinable) on the balance sheet.
  • Changes in fair value flow through investment income on the operating statement of the fund that holds them.
  • GASB 40 — requires footnote disclosure of investment policies and portfolio profiles.

Under the purchase method of accounting for inventory, when is an expenditure recognized?

Correct answer: a — The purchase method recognizes the full expenditure when inventory is acquired. The consumption method records expenditures only as inventory is actually used.

Barb City issues $5,000,000 in bonds for a new library. How is this recorded in the governmental fund?

Correct answer: c — Bond proceeds are an other financing source — not revenue — because they must be repaid. The long‑term liability only appears in the government‑wide statements.

Under the encumbrance‑lapses option, what two entries are made on July 1 of the new fiscal year?

Correct answer: b — Under the lapse option, the encumbrance is reestablished (budgetary entry) and the assigned fund balance reserved on June 30 is simultaneously released back to unassigned — the budgetary account now signals the commitment.
