Learning Objective 17‑3

Fund Types & Fund Accounting

Understand what a fund is, why governments use fund accounting, and the differences between the various fund types.

What is a fund?

A fund is a self-balancing set of accounts used to record the financial activity associated with a specific purpose or objective. Think of each fund as its own mini-accounting entity — it has its own assets, liabilities, revenues, and expenditures, completely separate from every other fund.

Governments use fund accounting because public money is rarely "general purpose." Tax revenue earmarked for road repair cannot legally be spent on police salaries. Grant money designated for a specific program must stay in that program. Fund accounting enforces these restrictions by keeping each pool of money in its own set of books.

A few key principles to keep in mind:

  • Each fund is an independent fiscal and accounting entity with its own self-balancing set of accounts.
  • Governments should use the minimum number of funds necessary — proliferating funds unnecessarily creates complexity without adding accountability.
  • There can only ever be one General Fund, but a government may have multiple special revenue funds, capital projects funds, and so on.
  • Transfers of resources between funds must be separately recorded and disclosed — they cannot simply be netted away.

Three categories of governmental activity

All governmental funds fall into one of three categories based on the nature of the activity they account for:

  • Governmental — activities that do not resemble commercial activities. Funded primarily through taxes and intergovernmental revenues rather than user charges.
  • Proprietary — activities that do resemble commercial activities; profitability or capital maintenance can be meaningfully measured. Funded primarily through fees charged to users.
  • Fiduciary — holding and managing assets that belong to others, such as pension assets or assets held in trust. The government is a steward, not the owner.

The fund families

Governmental Funds Account for basic public services. Only one General Fund may exist.

General fund

The primary operating fund; accounts for all activity not required to be in another fund (police, fire, parks, administration).

Special revenue funds

Revenues legally restricted to a specific purpose, such as federal grants or dedicated tax levies.

Capital projects funds

Financial resources designated for the acquisition or construction of major capital facilities.

Debt service funds

Accumulation of resources for, and the payment of, long-term debt principal and interest.

Permanent funds

Resources legally restricted so that only earnings — not principal — may be used for public purposes.

Proprietary Funds Operate like a business; costs recovered through user charges.

Enterprise fund

Services provided to the general public on a fee basis with the intent to recover costs — municipal utilities, transit systems, golf courses, sports arenas.

Internal service fund

Services provided by one department to other departments within the same government on a cost-reimbursement basis — vehicle pools, print shops, central purchasing.

Fiduciary Funds Resources held on behalf of others; cannot be used for the government's own operations.

Pension / employee benefit trust funds

Resources held for members and beneficiaries of defined benefit pension and other post-employment benefit plans.

Investment trust funds

External portions of investment pools operated by the government on behalf of other entities.

Private-purpose trust funds

All other trust arrangements that benefit individuals, private organizations, or other governments — not the reporting government itself.

Agency funds

Purely custodial — the government collects and remits resources but exercises no discretion over how they are used.

Examples of each fund type in practice

Quick illustrations using Barb City as our example government:

Governmental

  • General Fund — pays for police, fire, parks, and city administration funded by property and sales taxes
  • Special Revenue Fund — holds a federal housing grant restricted to low-income housing projects
  • Capital Projects Fund — receives bond proceeds earmarked for building a new public library; closed once construction is complete
  • Debt Service Fund — accumulates annual transfers to cover principal and interest payments on the library bonds
  • Permanent Fund — holds a $500,000 donor endowment; only investment earnings may be spent, and only on library books

Proprietary

  • Enterprise Fund — accounts for the municipal water and sewer utility, which bills residents monthly to recover operating costs
  • Internal Service Fund — runs the city motor pool; departments pay a monthly rate for vehicle access rather than maintaining their own fleets

Fiduciary

  • Pension Trust Fund — holds employee retirement contributions invested to pay future benefits; assets belong to the employees, not the city
  • Agency Fund — the county collects property taxes on behalf of local school districts and remits the proceeds; the county never owns these funds

Practice Question — The three major categories of governmental funds are:

Correct answer: c.

Which of the following is an example of a fiduciary fund?

Correct answer: b — Fiduciary funds hold resources that belong to others. Employee pension assets belong to the employees, not the city — making this a fiduciary fund.

A city's motor pool department charges other city departments a monthly fee for vehicle use. Which fund type accounts for this activity?

Correct answer: c — Internal service funds account for services provided by one department to other departments within the same government on a cost‑reimbursement basis.
