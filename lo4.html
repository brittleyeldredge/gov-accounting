Learning Objective 17‑4

Major Concepts of Governmental Accounting

Understand and explain major concepts of governmental accounting.

Elements of a statement of financial condition

Now that we understand the two layers of reporting (covered in LO 17‑3), we can look more closely at the elements that make up those statements. The statement of financial condition is the governmental equivalent of a balance sheet — it shows what the entity controls, what it owes, and what's left over.

  • Assets — resources with present service capacity that the entity presently controls. Examples: cash, property tax receivable, capital assets (in government-wide statements), prepaid expenses.
  • Liabilities — present obligations to sacrifice resources that the entity has little or no discretion to avoid. Examples: accounts payable, wages payable, bonds payable (in government-wide statements).
  • Net position — the residual of all other elements: Assets − Liabilities = Net Position. Replaces "equity" or "fund balance" in government-wide statements.
Assets − Liabilities = Net Position

Elements of the resource flow statements

The resource flow statement records what happened during the period — what came in and what went out. Governmental funds present this as the Statement of Revenues, Expenditures, and Changes in Fund Balance.

  • Inflow of resources — an acquisition of net assets applicable to the current reporting period. For governmental funds this means revenues that are measurable and available.
  • Outflow of resources — a consumption of net assets applicable to the current reporting period. For governmental funds this means expenditures — the cost of goods and services received in the current period payable from current resources.

Other financing sources (bond proceeds, interfund transfers in) and other financing uses (transfers out) are also reported here but are kept separate from revenues and expenditures so readers can see the difference.

The ending fund balance of $220,000 ties directly back to the balance sheet above — this is how the two statements connect. Also note: these are expenditures, not expenses. The distinction matters because governmental funds record the full cost of goods and services when received and payable, not when the underlying asset is consumed over time.

Expendability vs. capital maintenance

Commercial businesses ask "did we maintain our capital?" Governments ask "did we spend within budget on the public's behalf?" That difference shows up directly in the financial statements.

Commercial Enterprises
  • Measurement focus: flow of all economic resources
  • Method: accrual accounting
  • Balance sheet: current and noncurrent assets and liabilities; retained earnings reflects capital maintenance
Government Entities
  • Measurement focus: changes in current financial resources available to provide services per budget
  • Method: modified accrual
  • Balance sheet: only current assets, current liabilities, and a fund balance

Practice Question — Which of the following statements is true?

Correct answer: e — modified accrual + current financial resources is the governmental‑fund combination to memorize.

Which of the following correctly describes the modified accounting equation used for governmental fund balance sheets?

Correct answer: c — Governmental fund balance sheets use Assets − Liabilities = Fund Balance. Net position is used in government‑wide statements.

Under GASB 54, which fund balance classification represents resources formally committed by the highest level of governmental authority?

Correct answer: d — Limited (Committed) fund balance has been formally committed by the government’s highest decision‑making authority and can only be changed by that same level of authority.
