Learning Objective 17‑8

Interfund Activities

Make calculations and record journal entries for basic interfund activities.

When funds need to talk to each other

Up to this point we have looked at each fund as a self-contained accounting entity — its own revenues, its own expenditures, its own balance. But in practice, a government's funds don't operate in complete isolation. The general fund might lend money to a capital projects fund. A department in the general fund might rely on the motor pool's internal service fund for vehicles. The council might authorize a transfer from the general fund to the debt service fund to cover bond payments.

These cross-fund flows are called interfund activities, and they require careful accounting. Because each fund is its own fiscal entity, a transaction that moves resources from one fund to another must be recorded on both sides — it cannot simply be netted away or ignored. GASB 34 establishes four distinct categories of interfund activity, each treated differently depending on whether repayment is expected, whether a service is being rendered, or whether resources are simply being shifted from one pot to another.

Getting these right matters for accountability — users of governmental financial statements need to see exactly how resources move between funds to assess whether each fund is being managed within its legal constraints.

Resource flows between funds

Because each fund is its own independent fiscal entity, money moving between funds must be tracked and reported separately — it cannot be netted away. GASB 34 establishes four types of interfund activities:

Interfund Loans

One fund lends resources to another with repayment expected. The lending fund records a receivable; the borrowing fund records a payable.

Services Provided & Used

One fund "sells" goods or services to another at a price approximating cost or market (e.g., an internal service fund billing a department). Treated like a quasi‑external transaction.

Interfund Transfers

A flow of resources from one fund to another with no repayment expected. Reported as "other financing sources/uses," not as revenues or expenditures.

Interfund Reimbursements

Repayments from one fund to another for expenditures initially made on the other's behalf. The fund that originally paid reduces its expenditures; the reimbursing fund records the expenditure.

Why separate reporting matters

Governmental accounting requires the separate maintenance and reporting of interfund items because:

  • Each fund is an independent fiscal entity — pooling or netting would obscure each fund's accountability.
  • Users need to see how resources move between funds to assess whether each fund is managed within its legal restrictions.
  • Transfers that look like revenues or expenditures would mislead readers if not properly classified as financing activities.

Journal entries: Interfund Loans

The general fund lends $50,000 to the capital projects fund. Repayment is expected within the next fiscal year.

General Fund (lending)

Record the loan made
AccountDebitCredit
Due from Capital Projects Fund50,000
Cash50,000
When repaid
AccountDebitCredit
Cash50,000
Due from Capital Projects Fund50,000

Capital Projects Fund (borrowing)

Record the loan received
AccountDebitCredit
Cash50,000
Due to General Fund50,000
When repaid
AccountDebitCredit
Due to General Fund50,000
Cash50,000

The receivable and payable accounts (Due from and Due to) identify the specific fund on the other side of the transaction. If repayment is not expected within the current period, the receivable is reclassified as a non-current advance.

Journal entries: Services Provided & Used

The city's motor pool internal service fund bills the general fund $8,000 for vehicle services rendered during the month.

Internal Service Fund (providing)

Record the billing
AccountDebitCredit
Due from General Fund8,000
Revenue8,000
When payment received
AccountDebitCredit
Cash8,000
Due from General Fund8,000

General Fund (receiving services)

Record the expense/expenditure
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures8,000
Due to Internal Service Fund8,000
When payment made
AccountDebitCredit
Due to Internal Service Fund8,000
Cash8,000

Because the internal service fund operates like a business, it records revenue. The general fund treats the charge as an expenditure — the same as if it had purchased the service from an outside vendor.

Journal entries: Interfund Transfers

The city council authorizes a $45,000 transfer from the general fund to the debt service fund to cover upcoming bond payments. No repayment is expected.

General Fund (sending)

Record the transfer out
AccountDebitCredit
Other Financing Uses — Transfers Out45,000
Cash45,000

Debt Service Fund (receiving)

Record the transfer in
AccountDebitCredit
Cash45,000
Other Financing Sources — Transfers In45,000

Transfers are reported in the other financing sources and uses section of the operating statement — below revenues and expenditures — so readers can clearly see that this is a shift of resources between funds, not new revenue earned or a service purchased.

Journal entries: Interfund Reimbursements

The general fund mistakenly pays $8,000 in printing costs that should have been charged to the special revenue fund. The special revenue fund subsequently reimburses the general fund.

General Fund (originally paid)

Step 1 — General fund pays the bill
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures8,000
Cash8,000
Step 2 — Receives reimbursement; reverses expenditure
AccountDebitCredit
Cash8,000
Expenditures8,000

Special Revenue Fund (reimbursing)

Step 1 — No entry yet

The special revenue fund makes no entry until it reimburses the general fund.

Step 2 — Pays the reimbursement; records the expenditure
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures8,000
Cash8,000

After the reimbursement, the expenditure sits entirely in the special revenue fund where it belongs. The general fund's books are as if it never paid the bill. Unlike transfers, reimbursements are not reported as separate line items — they are simply a correction of where the expenditure is recorded.

The general fund transfers $45,000 to the debt service fund to cover bond payments. No repayment is expected. How should this be classified?

Correct answer: c — A flow of resources between funds with no repayment expected is an interfund transfer. It is reported as an other financing use in the sending fund and an other financing source in the receiving fund.

The general fund initially pays $8,000 for printing costs that should have been charged to the special revenue fund. The special revenue fund reimburses the general fund. How is this classified?

Correct answer: d — Interfund reimbursements occur when one fund pays on behalf of another and is later repaid. The paying fund reverses its expenditure; the reimbursing fund records the expenditure.
