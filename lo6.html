Learning Objective 17‑6

Budgeting Concepts

Understand and explain basic budgeting concepts in governmental accounting.

From understanding to doing

So far we have covered what funds are, how they're categorized, how the two layers of financial reporting work, the key concepts behind governmental accounting, and the measurement focus and basis of accounting that govern each fund type. Now we get into the actual accounting — how a government picks up a pencil and records what happens.

The place to start is the budget. In governmental accounting, the budget isn't just a management planning tool kept in a spreadsheet. It is recorded directly in the general ledger and carries the force of law. Recording the budget gives the government the legal authority to levy taxes, collect revenues, and spend money. That makes it the first journal entry of every fiscal year — and it shapes every transaction that follows.

A critical convention to know before we look at any entries: in this class, budget accounts are always written in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS. This distinguishes them from actual operating accounts at a glance — when you see a capitalized account name in a governmental journal entry, you know immediately it is a budgetary control account with no direct effect on reported revenues, expenditures, or fund balance. Actual accounts like Expenditures, Vouchers Payable, and Cash are written in normal title case. In practice, governments handle the budget convention differently. Some follow the same all-caps approach; others simply label accounts with the word "budgetary" (e.g., Budgetary Fund Balance instead of BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE). The underlying accounting is identical either way — it's purely a notational choice. For our purposes, we will always use caps for budget accounts.

Why budgets get journalized

In governmental accounting, the budget isn't just a planning spreadsheet — it's posted to the general ledger, providing the legal authority to levy taxes, collect revenue, and make expenditures.

Operating Budgets

Prepared for the general, special revenue, and debt service funds. Always recorded in the GL for control purposes.

Capital Budgets

Prepared for the capital projects fund. Also recorded for Capital Projects and Debt Service funds when useful.

Key vocabulary

  • Appropriation — the legal authority to expend resources. Annual budgets for the General Fund and Special Revenue Funds are always recorded in the GL.
  • Encumbrance — a commitment related to an unperformed (executory) contract for goods or services. Recorded in special GL accounts to prevent overspending the appropriation.

Budget entries have no effect on reported operations — they are a control mechanism, not a record of economic events.

The budget → expenditure pipeline

Every dollar a government spends follows a predictable path from legislative approval to cash leaving the bank. Understanding each stage helps make sense of why the journal entries look the way they do.

Appropriation (Budget Recorded)→ Encumbrance→ Expenditure→ Disbursement
Stage 1 — Appropriation (Budget Recorded)

At the start of the fiscal year, the governing body formally adopts the budget and it is journalized into the general ledger. Recording the budget is the appropriation — they are one and the same event. It sets the legal spending authority for the year. ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL is debited and APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL is credited.

Stage 2 — Encumbrance

When a department places an order or signs a contract, it records an encumbrance — a reservation of part of the appropriation. No cash has moved and no goods have arrived, but the commitment is on the books. This prevents the department from spending the same appropriation twice. ENCUMBRANCES is debited and BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENCUMBRANCES is credited.

Stage 3 — Expenditure

When goods arrive or services are rendered, the encumbrance is reversed and the actual expenditure is recorded. Unlike commercial accounting where costs are expensed as consumed, governmental funds record the full expenditure when the liability is incurred and payable from current resources.

Stage 4 — Disbursement

The final stage is the cash payment — Vouchers Payable is debited and Cash is credited. Many governments use a voucher system requiring formal approval before cash is released, adding an extra layer of control over public funds.

Recording the operating budget

On July 1, 20X1, Barb City's council approves $900,000 in estimated revenue and $850,000 in appropriations for the general fund.

July 1, 20X1 — Record general fund budget for year
AccountDebitCredit
ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL900,000
APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL850,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—UNASSIGNED50,000
  • ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL is an anticipatory asset.
  • APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL is an anticipatory liability.
  • The $50,000 surplus is credited to BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—UNASSIGNED. A projected deficit would be debited there instead.

Worked example: what happens throughout the year

Once the budget is recorded on July 1, the city goes about its business. Departments order supplies, sign contracts, receive goods, and pay invoices — all within the boundaries set by the appropriation. Here's how that plays out for one purchase: within the $850,000 appropriation, $15,000 is set aside for a chipper machine. The actual invoice comes in at $14,000.

Step 1 — Record the encumbrance / August 3, 20X1 — Purchase order issued

August 3, 20X1 — Chipper machine ordered; reserve $15,000 from the appropriation
AccountDebitCredit
ENCUMBRANCES15,000
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000
Authority after Aug 3: $850,000 − $15,000 encumbrance = $835,000 remaining

Step 2 — Goods received: reverse the encumbrance / October 11, 20X1 — Machine delivered, invoice received

October 11, 20X1 — Reverse the full $15,000 encumbrance
AccountDebitCredit
BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE—ASSIGNED FOR ENC.15,000
ENCUMBRANCES15,000

Step 3 — Record the actual expenditure / October 11, 20X1 — Same day as delivery

October 11, 20X1 — Record expenditure at actual invoice cost of $14,000
AccountDebitCredit
Expenditures14,000
Vouchers Payable14,000
Authority after Oct 11: $850,000 − $14,000 expenditure − $0 open encumbrances = $836,000 remaining Note: encumbrance freed up $15,000 but expenditure consumed $14,000 — the $1,000 difference returns to available authority.

Step 4 — Pay the voucher / November 1, 20X1 — Payment processed

November 1, 20X1 — Disburse cash to vendor
AccountDebitCredit
Vouchers Payable14,000
Cash14,000

The encumbrance always reverses at its original amount ($15,000) even though the actual expenditure was only $14,000.

November 18, 20X1 — Where does the full budget stand?

The chipper machine is just one of many purchases made throughout the year. By November 18, Barb City has recorded $400,000 in total expenditures (including the $14,000 chipper) and still has $30,000 in open encumbrances for other outstanding orders.

$850,000 − ($400,000 + $30,000) = $420,000 remaining authority

Both expenditures and open encumbrances are subtracted from the appropriation — a committed but undelivered order is just as much a claim on the budget as a paid invoice.

Practice Question — Why do state and local governments record encumbrances?

Correct answer: a — encumbrances prevent the entity from overspending its legal appropriation.

On July 1, 20X1, Barb City records its budget: $900,000 in estimated revenues and $850,000 in appropriations. Which entry is correct?

Correct answer: d — The budget entry debits ESTIMATED REVENUES CONTROL and credits APPROPRIATIONS CONTROL. The $50,000 surplus (revenues exceed appropriations) goes to BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE — UNASSIGNED.

As of November 18, Barb City has $850,000 in appropriations, $400,000 in expenditures, and $30,000 in open encumbrances. How much appropriating authority remains?

Correct answer: b — $850,000 − ($400,000 + $30,000) = $420,000. Both expenditures and open encumbrances reduce available authority.

When a purchase order is issued for $10,000 of supplies, which entry is recorded?

Correct answer: c — An encumbrance is recorded when a purchase order is issued — before goods arrive. ENCUMBRANCES is debited and BUDGETARY FUND BALANCE — ASSIGNED FOR ENC. is credited.
