Learning Objective 17‑5

Measurement Focus & Basis of Accounting

Understand and explain the basic differences in measurement focus and basis of accounting between governmental and private sector accounting.

Three types of funds, three different accounting approaches

As we saw in LO 17‑2, governmental entities use three categories of funds — governmental, proprietary, and fiduciary. One of the most important things to understand is that these categories don't all account for their activity the same way. The rules for when to recognize revenues and expenditures, and what to measure, differ meaningfully across the three.

Governmental funds are the focus of this course, and they work differently from anything you've seen in financial accounting. Their purpose isn't to run like a business — it's to deliver public services within legal spending authority. What matters most is whether the right amount of spendable resources came in and went out in the right period. That calls for a unique approach: modified accrual, focused only on current financial resources.

Proprietary funds (enterprise and internal service) are worth knowing about, but they operate much like a private business — charging fees and aiming to recover costs. Because of this, they use the same full accrual basis you already learned in financial accounting, recognizing revenues when earned and expenses when incurred regardless of when cash moves.

Fiduciary funds hold assets on behalf of others and similarly use full accrual, since an accurate picture of all economic resources is needed to properly account for obligations to beneficiaries like pension plan members.

In this course we will focus primarily on governmental funds and modified accrual accounting. Proprietary and fiduciary fund accounting follow principles you already know from financial accounting, so it's worth being aware of the distinction — but the unique rules of governmental accounting are where our attention will be.

Two key terms

  • Measurement focus — what flows should be measured for operations.
  • Basis of accounting — when transactions and events should be recognized in the financial statements.
Governmental Activities
  • Measure flow of current financial resources
  • Modified accrual basis
  • Presents Statement of Revenues, Expenditures & Changes in Fund Balance
  • Shows the change in net financial resources available for near‑term spending
Proprietary & Fiduciary Activities
  • Measure flow of all economic resources
  • Accrual basis
  • Presents Statement of Revenues & Expenses
  • Also presents a Statement of Cash Flows

Government‑wide financial statements are always on the accrual basis regardless of fund type.

Which funds use which basis?

Modified Accrual — Governmental Funds
  • General Fund
  • Special Revenue Funds
  • Capital Projects Funds
  • Debt Service Funds
  • Permanent Funds
Accrual — Proprietary & Fiduciary Funds
  • Enterprise Fund
  • Internal Service Fund
  • Trust Funds (all 3 types)
  • Agency Funds

Current financial resources

Current Financial Resources Include

Cash, property tax receivables, prepaids, and supplies inventories.

Claims Against Them Include

Wages, payroll taxes, vendor payables, and other liabilities expected to be paid within 60 days after year‑end.

Modified accrual: recognition rules

  • Revenue — recorded when measurable (amount can be objectively determined) and available (collected within the current period, or expected within 60 days of fiscal year‑end to pay current‑period liabilities).
  • Expenditures — recognized when related liabilities are measurable and incurred and are payable out of current financial resources.

Practice Question — The modified accrual basis of accounting:

Correct answer: c — available + measurable for revenue; measurable + incurred for expenditures.

Property taxes are levied on January 1, 20X2 for the fiscal year ending June 30, 20X2 and collected on March 15, 20X2. Under modified accrual, when should revenue be recognized?

Correct answer: a — Under modified accrual, revenues are recognized when measurable and available. Taxes levied on January 1 are measurable at that point and will be collected within the fiscal year.

Which of the following funds uses full accrual accounting and measures the flow of all economic resources?

Correct answer: c — Enterprise funds are proprietary funds and use full accrual with an all‑economic‑resources measurement focus — just like a private business.
